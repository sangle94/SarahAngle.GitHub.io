Mimicking Robotic Hand Exoskeleton

This work is a collaboration of three engineering students. We created a teaching aid, which allows for precision and distance learning of physical tasks. A soft trasmitting glove captures a teacher's motion, while servos guide the exoskeleton user's hand to recreate this movement - in real time. Customized with a 3D scanner and created with a 3D printer, the exoskeleton is fit to the user's hand.

This project was developed as part of an internship at Autodesk, and created at Autodesk's San Francisco Pier 9 facility.

Motor Development for Soft Robotics

I developed and researched a new material to be used as a motor in soft robotics with a PhD student. We found that the material expands as it is heated by thermal or electric means. I harnessed and characterized this process's extension and force using a custom built testing apparatus.

This research was conducted at the Cornell Creative Machines Lab under Professor Hod Lipson.

Autonomous Robot Competition

I built a microcontroller based autonomous battle bot . Created with two other students, the robot incorporated QTI and radar sensors. We programmed a custom battle strategy in C, controlling motors as well as an offensive weapon. We also designed and manufactured a chassis and defensive shell using a laser cutter.

The Hurricane was a product developed in Cornell's MAE 2250 Class. I created it as a fun way to mix and visualize drinks. Designed in four weeks and manufactured using 3D printers, the Hurricane has a price point under $100 - for a production run of 1 or 10,000.

Design and Build: Air Motor

As part of Cornell's MAE 2250 class, myself and three other sophomore engineering student quickly designed and built an air motor in five weeks with a budget of $50. Every part was designed and animated in SolidWorks. The small air motor (think shoebox sized) was built completely in house from aluminum and steel. We ran engineering analyses on the motor to test for safety factor and power output.
